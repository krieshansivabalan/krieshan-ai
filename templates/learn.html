✦ What is Sidereal Astrology?

Sidereal astrology is the original star-based system — it tracks planetary positions against the actual backdrop of the fixed stars. The word sidereal comes from the Latin sidus, meaning star. This is the system used by ancient Babylonian, Egyptian, and Vedic (Indian) astrologers for thousands of years.

The key idea is straightforward: when sidereal astrology says "the Sun is in Aries," it means the Sun is actually in the region of sky occupied by the constellation Aries. The planets are measured against real star positions, not an abstract mathematical point.

"The sidereal zodiac is a star map. The tropical zodiac is a season map. Both are meaningful — they measure different things."

Today, Vedic (Jyotish) astrology is the primary living tradition that uses the sidereal zodiac. It also forms the basis of Western sidereal astrology, pioneered in the 20th century by Cyril Fagan and Donald Bradley.

↔ Tropical vs Sidereal

Western astrology (the kind in newspaper horoscopes) uses the tropical zodiac. This system anchors 0° Aries to the Spring Equinox — the moment when day and night are equal in the Northern Hemisphere. The tropical zodiac is a calendar — it reflects seasons, not star positions.

Sidereal astrology anchors 0° Aries to the actual Aries constellation. Because the Earth wobbles on its axis (a phenomenon called precession of the equinoxes), the Spring Equinox drifts backward through the constellations at about 50 arc-seconds per year — roughly 1° every 72 years.

🌱 Tropical Zodiac

Anchored to the Spring Equinox. Measures the annual solar cycle and seasons. The most widely known system in Western cultures. 0° Aries = March 20–21 each year, regardless of where the stars are.

⭐ Sidereal Zodiac

Anchored to actual star positions. Currently about 23–24° behind the tropical zodiac. Most people find their sidereal Sun sign is one sign earlier than their tropical Sun sign.

The gap between the two systems is currently about 23–24 degrees (depending on the ayanamsa used). This means if your tropical chart says "Sun in Aries," your sidereal chart likely shows "Sun in Pisces." Neither is wrong — they measure different things.

∿ Ayanamsa Systems

The ayanamsa (from Sanskrit: ayana = movement, amsa = portion) is the correction value subtracted from tropical longitudes to get sidereal positions. It represents the accumulated precession since the two zodiacs were last aligned.

Lahiri (IAU Official)

The Indian government standard, adopted in 1955. Most widely used by Vedic and Western sidereal astrologers. Currently ~23.85°. Best starting point for most people.

Fagan-Bradley

Developed by Cyril Fagan and Donald Bradley. About 0.9° larger than Lahiri. Popular among Western sidereal astrologers following the Babylonian tradition. Currently ~24.72°.

Krishnamurti (KP)

Refined by K.S. Krishnamurti for his KP system of event prediction. Nearly identical to Lahiri but uses a slightly different reference epoch. Favored for precise timing.

True Chitrapaksha

Dynamically keeps the star Spica (Chitra) at exactly 180° sidereal. Because Spica moves slightly, this ayanamsa changes more than a fixed formula. Used by astronomically precise practitioners.

☿ The 9 Planets (Navagrahas)

♈ The 12 Signs in Sidereal Framing

In sidereal astrology, the 12 signs carry the same archetypal meanings as in Western astrology — but their timing shifts back ~23 days. The sign energies are tied to stellar positions, not seasons.

♈ Aries — The Pioneer

Courage, initiative, raw energy. The cosmic first breath. Ruled by Mars.

♉ Taurus — The Builder

Embodiment, beauty, resource, patience. The cosmic cultivator. Ruled by Venus.

♊ Gemini — The Connector

Intelligence, curiosity, duality, communication. The cosmic weaver. Ruled by Mercury.

♋ Cancer — The Nurturer

Emotion, home, intuition, memory, protection. The cosmic mother. Ruled by Moon.

♌ Leo — The Sovereign

Radiance, creativity, self-expression, authority. The cosmic king. Ruled by Sun.

Discernment, service, craft, healing, analysis. The cosmic healer. Ruled by Mercury.

Depth, power, death/rebirth, intensity, occult. The cosmic alchemist. Ruled by Mars/Ketu.

☽ The 27 Nakshatras

The nakshatras (lunar mansions) divide the zodiac into 27 equal segments of 13°20' each. The Moon transits one nakshatra roughly every day. In Vedic astrology, your natal Moon's nakshatra is considered more revealing than your Moon sign — it provides finer psychological and karmic detail.

Each nakshatra has a ruling planet (lord), a deity, an animal symbol, and a characteristic energy. The four padas (quarters) of each nakshatra correspond to the four aims of life: Dharma, Artha, Kama, Moksha.

⏳ Vimshottari Dasha System

The Vimshottari Dasha is Vedic astrology's primary timing system. It divides life into planetary periods (Maha Dashas) totaling 120 years, determined by your natal Moon's nakshatra position.

The system works like this: each nakshatra has a ruling planet (lord). The planet ruling your Moon's nakshatra at birth governs your first Maha Dasha. The balance of that dasha is calculated based on how far the Moon has traveled through the nakshatra.

Dasha Sequence & Duration

Ketu — 7 years
Venus — 20 years
Sun — 6 years
Moon — 10 years
Mars — 7 years
Rahu — 18 years
Jupiter — 16 years
Saturn — 19 years
Mercury — 17 years

Antar Dasha (Sub-Periods)

Each Maha Dasha is divided into 9 sub-periods (Antar Dashas), proportional to each planet's dasha length within the total 120-year cycle. So a Jupiter Maha Dasha (16 years) begins with a Jupiter-Ketu sub-period, then Jupiter-Venus, and so on.

The Dasha system is considered one of Vedic astrology's most powerful tools for understanding when life events unfold. Each planet's dasha activates its natal house, sign, and aspects — bringing the themes of that planet to the foreground of lived experience.

📖 Glossary
Ayanamsa
The angular difference between the tropical and sidereal zodiacs at any given moment. Calculated by measuring the accumulated precession of the equinoxes since a chosen reference date. Different ayanamsa systems use different reference epochs.
Graha
Sanskrit for "planet" or "seizer." In Vedic astrology, refers to the nine planetary bodies: Sun, Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Rahu, and Ketu. Each graha "seizes" aspects of life under its domain.
Nakshatra
One of 27 lunar mansions that divide the 360° zodiac into equal 13°20' segments. The Moon transits one nakshatra approximately every day. Your natal Moon's nakshatra is considered one of the most personally significant placements in Vedic astrology.
Dasha
A planetary period in the Vimshottari timing system. The Maha Dasha is the major period (6–20 years) and the Antar Dasha is the sub-period within it. Your natal Moon's nakshatra lord determines which dasha you are born into.
Rahu & Ketu
The North and South lunar nodes — the points where the Moon's orbit crosses the ecliptic. Not physical planets, but shadow points of immense karmic significance. Rahu governs present-life desire and dharma; Ketu governs past-life karma and liberation. They always sit exactly opposite each other.
Kuta
In Vedic synastry, Kuta refers to the eight compatibility factors assessed when comparing two charts. The maximum total score is 36 points. A score of 18+ is generally considered acceptable for partnership; 28+ is considered excellent.
Fixed Star
A star outside our solar system that appears stationary relative to other stars over human timescales (in contrast to planets, which "wander"). When a natal planet is conjunct a major fixed star within ~1°, the star's qualities intensify and modify that planet's expression.
Whole Sign Houses
The oldest house system in astrology. The rising sign's full 30° extent becomes the entire 1st house; the next sign becomes the entire 2nd house; and so on. Favored in Vedic astrology for its elegance and clarity.
Pada
Each of the four equal quarters of a nakshatra (3°20' each). The four padas correspond to the four aims of life: Dharma (1st), Artha (2nd), Kama (3rd), Moksha (4th).
Jyotish
The traditional Sanskrit name for Vedic astrology, meaning "science of light" or "the eye of the Vedas." One of the six Vedangas (limbs of Vedic knowledge), used traditionally for both natal interpretation and election of auspicious times for action.
